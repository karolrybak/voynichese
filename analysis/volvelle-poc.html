How it works

Three concentric disks turn on one pin. A radial window at the top exposes one sector of each ring; read them outer→inner and you get a word: prefix + core + suffix. The rings are geared: the prefix you land on only lets certain core sectors line up under the window, and the core only lets certain suffixes line up (dimmed sectors are locked out). Occasionally the scribe doesn’t spin at all — they glance at a nearby word and copy it with a small change.

Why this exact design (it’s forced by the data)

Measured in the manuscriptFeature of the device
Words = fixed prefix·core·suffix shape; a tiny inventory covers the corpus Three rings with ~10 sectors each
Binomial word length (variance/mean < 1; natural languages are > 1) Core ring read 0–2 times → a few independent draws → binomial length
All 5 scribes share the same statistics & affix inventory They each held a copy of the same wheel
Currier “languages” A/B, per-scribe differences A different disk, or a favoured set of sectors
Very low conditional entropy — the next character is almost determined by the previous one (sequential, not positional) Geared rings: each ring constrains the next. A free grille (independent windows) over-produces — our simulation shows its entropy is far too high
Extra repeated / near-repeated words (self-citation) beyond what any independent generator makes Not the device — the human copying a neighbouring word (toggle above)

The geared requirement is the key result: a plain Cardan-grille-over-a-table (independent windows) reproduces word length and the affix list but emits character combinations the manuscript never uses. Coupling the rings fixes it. Medieval combinatorial wheels — Ramon Llull’s Ars Magna — were exactly such geared concentric disks, and the manuscript itself draws volvelles (the rosette pages).

This page is a concept demo with hand-set rings for legibility. The quantitative version (rings learned from the corpus, matched against the real text) is volvelle.ts / generate.ts; see the repository README.
